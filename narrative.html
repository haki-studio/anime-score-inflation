Does an 8.5 Today Mean What It Did in 2010?

Every anime fan has had some version of this argument. Someone calls a new show a masterpiece, an 8.5, a top-tier season, and someone older rolls their eyes and says scores don't mean what they used to - that everything gets an 8.5 now, that the number has been inflated into meaninglessness. It's a familiar complaint, and like a lot of familiar complaints it's usually asserted, never tested. So I tested it. I pulled the scores for every TV anime on AniList going back to 2000 - 3,476 titles1 - and asked a simple question with a not-so-simple answer: has the meaning of a score drifted over time?

The short version: no, not in the way the eye-rollers mean. The average score of a popular anime is essentially flat over fifteen years, and a genuine 8.5 is still rare. But there's a smaller, realer thing buried underneath the complaint, and that's where it gets interesting - because "score inflation" turns out to be three different claims that don't all point the same way, and separating them is the whole game.

Why you can't just plot the average

The lazy way to answer this is to chart the average score by year and see if the line goes up. I'll show you that line, but I want to flag why it can't be the whole answer, because this is exactly where amateur analysis goes wrong.

A rising yearly average could mean anime genuinely got better. It could mean the raters changed - AniList's user base grew enormously over this period, and a bigger, more casual, more fandom-driven crowd rates differently than the small core of early adopters did. Or it could be survivorship and selection: more shows get made now, the catalog's composition shifted, and the mix of what's even getting scored is different. The average moving doesn't tell you which of these is happening. So instead of one line, I ran three tests that each isolate a different piece, and trusted the answer only where they agreed.

Test one: the raw trend

Across the full window, the mean score of a popular TV anime went from 69.78 in the late 2000s (2005-09) to 69.58 in the early 2020s (2020-24) - a shift of -0.2 points. That is, for all intents and purposes, no change at all. Two-tenths of a point on a 100-point scale, in the wrong direction for the inflation story, across fifteen years. The typical well-watched anime is rated almost exactly the same today as it was when people were arguing about Code Geass.

That's the headline the eye-rollers don't expect, but per the warning above, a flat average isn't the end of the story either - an average can sit still while the shape underneath it moves. We need the tests that look at specific points in the distribution rather than its center.

Test two: where does a fixed "8.5" sit?

This is the cleaner test. Forget the average; track one fixed score across time and watch its rank. If an 8.5 (an 85 on AniList's 100-point scale) ranked at, say, the 90th percentile of releases in 2008 - better than 90% of that year's shows - but only the 75th percentile today, then the same number has slid down the standings. That's inflation in its purest form: the score didn't change, but the company it keeps got better, so it means less.

In the data, an 8.5 sat at roughly the 98th percentile in 2005-09 and the 96th percentile in 2020-24 - a drift of -1.6 points. So it did slide, but barely. An 85 used to mean "better than about 97.6% of that year's shows"; now it means "better than about 96%." It went from being roughly the top 1-in-40 show to the top 1-in-25. That's a real loosening, but it's a small one, and notice where it leaves us: an 8.5 is still an unambiguously elite score, the kind a year produces a handful of times. The claim that "everything's an 8.5 now" is just false. An 8.5 remains rare. It's a slightly less exclusive club than it was, not a turnstile.

Test three: what does it take to be elite now?

The last test asks the same question from the top down: what score do you need to crack the top 10% of releases in a given year? If that cutoff is climbing, the bar for "great" is rising - more shows are clustered up high, so you need a bigger number to stand out. The top-10% cutoff moved from 78.48 in the late 2000s to 81.0 in the early 2020s, a shift of +2.52 points. This is the one test that moves a meaningful amount. To be a top-decile show in 2008 you needed about a 78.5; today you need an 81. The bar to be considered "very good" has risen two and a half points.

And there's a tell underneath it: the spread of scores widened. The standard deviation of a year's scores ran around 7.3 in the late 2000s and climbed to roughly 9 to 10 in the late 2010s and early 2020s. The distribution didn't shift up; it fanned out. There are more shows piled up in the low 80s now and more down in the basement than there used to be.

Putting it together

So do the three tests agree? Not on the simple story, and that's the finding. If scores had broadly inflated, all three would move together: the mean would rise, a fixed 8.5 would slide a lot, and the top-decile cutoff would climb. Instead the mean is flat, the 8.5 slid only a hair, and only the top-decile cutoff moved with any conviction. The most direct measure of "scores went up" - the average - says they didn't.

What actually happened is subtler and more specific than "inflation." The center of the distribution held still while the shape around it changed. The bar to be merely very good rose about two and a half points, the scores spread out, and a denser cluster of shows now sits in the low 80s than used to. Crossing into genuine 8.5 territory, though, is only marginally easier than it was. Put plainly: there are more "an 8" shows floating around now, which is exactly the texture the eye-roller is reacting to - but that pile-up is happening in the low-80s "very good" band, not at the 85-and-up "masterpiece" line, and it coexists with more low-scored shows, not fewer. The vibe the complaint is built on is real. The conclusion it jumps to - that the whole scale inflated and an 8.5 is meaningless - is not.

There's a methodological reason to trust the "no broad inflation" read even more than the raw numbers suggest. Popularity here is measured today, not at release2 - so the "popular" shows from 2005-09 are the old titles that stayed popular enough to still clear 5,000 user lists, which means they're survivors, positively selected by fifteen years of word of mouth. That selection should flatter the old years. And the old years still don't score higher than the recent ones. If anything, the comparison is tilted against finding inflation, and it still doesn't find it.

What this means for the argument you were having

Here's the practical upshot. You do not need to mentally dock a couple of points off a 2024 score to compare it fairly to a 2010 one. The average hasn't moved, and a real 8.5 means almost exactly what it meant fifteen years ago - it's still the rare, top-of-the-year score it always was. So when your friend calls this season's standout an 8.5, the person rolling their eyes is wrong on the merits, and now you've got the numbers to say so.

The one place to keep your guard up is the tier just below that. A low-80s score - the "this is really good" band - is genuinely more common now than it used to be, because that's where the distribution thickened. So the skepticism that the eye-rollers want to spray across the whole scale is actually warranted in exactly one narrow spot: the gap between "very good" (low 80s) and "elite" (85+) is wider and more crowded than it looks, and an 82 today is a slightly cheaper signal than an 82 was in 2010. An 8.5, though? That one still earned it.
